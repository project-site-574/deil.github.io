DEIL: Depth-Estimated Imitation Learning

Anonymous RA-L Submission

Abstract

Point cloud–based imitation learning has emerged as a powerful approach for robotic manipulation, enabling policies to reason explicitly about 3D geometry. In such pipelines, depth image acquisition plays a critical role in point cloud construction. However, raw depth from RGB-D cameras is often noisy, sensitive to transparent, reflective, and repetitive surfaces, and fundamentally limited by a minimum sensing distance.

Recent advances in learned depth estimation offer a promising alternative: modern monocular, stereo, and multi-view approaches have achieved impressive performance in vision benchmarks. However, robotics still lacks a systematic understanding of how these advances translate to end-to-end policy learning and execution.

In this work, based on our introduced Depth-Estimated Imitation Learning (DEIL) general framework, we present the first systematic study of how modern depth estimation impacts point cloud–based imitation learning across two paradigms: Behaviour Cloning and In-Context Imitation Learning. Using 3D Diffusion Policy and Instant Policy as representative frameworks, we evaluate depth sensing alongside learned monocular, stereo, and multi-view depth pipelines through their downstream effect on robot performance. Our results show that depth estimation approaches could narrow the gap to depth images from depth cameras. Moreover, it delivers significant gains in real-world manipulation tasks under challenging visual conditions.

DEIL: Depth-Estimated Imitation Learning

To systematically study the role of depth estimation in imitation learning—particularly under challenging perceptual conditions—we propose a general framework DEIL. The framework consists of three main components: (1) a depth estimation module, (2) a point cloud reconstruction module, and (3) an imitation learning module. This modular design allows us to plug in different depth estimation methods while holding the learning framework fixed, enabling controlled evaluation of their impact on robot policy performance.

First, in the depth estimation stage, raw RGB images are passed through a depth estimator to produce corresponding depth maps. Second, using the camera intrinsics and extrinsics, unscaled point clouds are reconstructed from each camera view, and a Scaled ICP method is applied to align and merge them into a unified representation. Third, point cloud–based behaviour cloning or in-context imitation learning methods are trained on the reconstructed 3D representations generated by the previous modules.

Results

Simulation Results

To select a strong and practical configuration for each depth estimator, we first conduct a preliminary hyperparameter study in simulation. It is carried out in the ICIL paradigm, which is lightweight and allows rapid iteration over perception configurations. Specifically, we evaluate different combinations of the above hyperparameters on 10 manipulation tasks from RLBench and identify a set of optimised depth estimation configurations that yield strong performance for each depth estimator. For each task and configuration, we collect 5 demos. Then, we condition each demo as a single context and run Instant Policy for 2 rollouts, recording the average success rate for each setting. After that, we average across 10 tasks to obtain the final results, which is reported in Appendix I.

Real-World Results

We evaluate the performance of different depth estimation methods on a suite of real-world manipulation tasks under challenging visual conditions, including transparent, reflective, and textureless objects.
